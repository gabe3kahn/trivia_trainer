6b. WORDPLAY (every run, in addition to the two sourced categories). Generate **10 constructed wordplay clues using exactly TWO of the SEVEN mechanics below — 5 of each** — and **rotate which two you use across runs** so every mechanic gets coverage over time (check recent `data/sourcing/packs/drafts/wordplay-*.json` for the last pairing and pick a different one). **Construct** them (never source). Each clue: `source: "constructed"`, `verification_status: "skipped"`, `category_id: "language_wordplay"`, `citations: []`, a `wp` block for the validator, and a `subcategory_name` set to the mechanic's EXACT subcategory — **anagram → "Anagrams", before_after → "Before & After", homophone → "Homophones & Soundalikes", hidden_word → "Hidden Words", initials → "Initials & Abbreviations", crossword → "Crossword Clues", rhyme_time → "Rhyme Time"**. **Answer casing:** write **crossword** answers in UPPERCASE (they fill a letter grid) and **every other** wordplay answer in Title Case (`Elbow`, `Ant`, `Golden Gate Crasher`) — never all-caps for non-crossword. (A deterministic step re-normalizes casing after you write, so it's self-correcting — but follow the rule so the draft reads right.) **The no-answer-leak rule applies to EVERY mechanic** — the clue must never contain the answer's words or a word sharing a stem. Mechanics:
   - **anagram** (`wp: {"scramble": "<word>"}`): the answer is an anagram of the scramble word. Write a natural, witty sentence where **the answer's MEANING completes the thought** and the scramble word sits naturally elsewhere in that same sentence — the solver must spot which ordinary word to rearrange. **NEVER a fill-in-the-blank / "___"** (it telegraphs the answer) and **NEVER call the word out** (no bold, CAPS, italics, underline, or quotes). The scramble must be a DIFFERENT word from the answer, **never a near-synonym** of it (angered/enraged is too easy), and you must **not reuse a pair already used** in any existing `data/sourcing/packs/drafts/wordplay-*.json` (e.g. silent/listen, night/thing — open them and check first). `constraint_text`: "Rearrange the letters of one word in this clue to find the answer." Good: *"Don't leave your arm dangling below the table — just bend the hinge joint to wave hello"* → **Elbow** (scramble "below").
   - **before_after** (`wp: {"first": "<A>", "second": "<B>"}`): A and B share a pivot word and the answer is the splice; one narrative clues both halves **without naming either**. **A and B must be CONCEPTUALLY UNRELATED — joined ONLY by a coincidental shared pivot word, never by a shared theme.** Test before writing: *would these two phrases ever co-occur outside this pun?* If they share a domain it's not a real Before & After (*Sugar Cane* + *Cane Toad* both belong to the cane-toad story — BAD; *Cane Toad* + *Toadstool*, an amphibian and a fungus — GOOD). And the spliced composite must **not read as a real standalone term** (*Ground Floor Plan*, *Music Box Office*, *Short Circuit Board* are real things, not surprising splices — BAD). Good: *"San Francisco's iconic suspension bridge leads to an uninvited party guest"* → **Golden Gate Crasher** (a bridge and a party guest — unrelated, spliced on "Gate").
   - **homophone** (`wp: {"homophone": "<soundalike>"}`): the answer sounds like the soundalike but is spelled differently; weave the soundalike naturally into a SCENE and let the answer's meaning complete it. **Write a scene, do NOT point at or define the answer** (same discipline as hidden_word — a clue that describes the answer directly is "too pointed" and fails review). Bad (too pointed): *"this spinning arrow atop barns indicates wind direction"* → Vane — it names exactly what a weather vane does. Good (answer **Vane**, soundalike "vain"): *"Too vain to ever hold still, the copper rooster on the barn's peak swung to face each new gust off the fields."* (weaves in 'vain,' paints a scene, never says 'weather vane' or 'wind direction'). Good (answer **Leek**, soundalike "leak"): *"A leak in the galley ceiling dripped steadily onto the cutting board where the cook was chopping his vegetables."* Make sure they genuinely sound alike (not auto-checked).
   - **hidden_word** (`wp: {"hidden_in": "<carrier phrase>"}`): the answer hides as consecutive letters inside a longer phrase that appears in the clue (e.g. "cat" inside "lo**cat**ion") — but never visually mark it. **The whole game is that the hidden word is a SURPRISE, so two rules are non-negotiable: (1) the carrier must be an ORDINARY word whose meaning is UNRELATED to the answer** (hiding "ant" in "eleph**ant**" or "ice" in "off**ice**" is good; hiding it in a word that's on-topic gives it away), **and (2) the clue must NOT separately define or describe the answer** — write a scene, not a definition. Bad: *"this six-legged insect marches in a single-file pro**cess**ion"* → Ant (the clue defines "ant", so the hide is pointless). Good: *"The eleph**ant** loomed like a mountain over the tiny insect scuttling between its feet"* → **Ant** (nothing defines an ant; the letters are the only path in).
   - **initials** (`wp: {"initials": "<e.g. S.S.>"}`): a trivia clue whose answer's INITIALS are the hint (multi-word answer; the validator checks the initials = the first letter of each answer word, and every alias must match too). **The initials are a DISAMBIGUATOR — so write the clue deliberately VAGUER than a standalone clue and lean on the initials to pin the one answer.** A clue that already determines the answer by itself wastes the mechanic and plays far too easy (reviewers knock those to $200). **The test: strip the initials — the clue should have SEVERAL plausible answers; add them back — exactly one fits.** Because you give a structural hint, **pitch HARDER than the dollar tier suggests** — the prose alone should under-determine the answer. Never name the answer's words or leak a partial. GOOD (vague alone, resolved by the hint): *"An Avenger's given name — B.B."* → **Bruce Banner** ("an Avenger's given name" is hopelessly ambiguous until B.B. resolves it). BAD (self-determining, hint redundant → too easy): *"Awarded annually to the champion of North America's top professional ice-hockey league — S.C."* → Stanley Cup — the prose alone already screams the answer.
   - **crossword** (`wp: {"length": <n>, "pattern": "<e.g. ___S___>"}`): a clue plus the answer's letter count and ONE revealed letter (rendered "(7) _ _ _ S _ _ _"). **The length + letter are DISAMBIGUATORS — so write the clue VAGUER (a broad synonym or loose definition is fine) and let the grid pin the answer; pitch HARDER than the dollar tier.** The test: without the grid the clue should fit several answers; with it, exactly one. Rules: answer **≥4 letters**, `length` equals it exactly, pattern reveals **exactly one letter** (a middle/later letter is harder than the first); every alias must also fit the length + pattern. **The clue's grammar must pin the answer's EXACT form** — a plural answer needs a plural clue (*"Sour green citrus fruits"* → LIMES, never *"…fruit"* → LIME), a past-tense answer a past-tense clue (*"What the barista did to the beans"* → GROUND, never GRIND). Never name the answer or leak a word of it. GOOD (vague alone, resolved by the grid): *"Amazed"*, `{"length":4,"pattern":"_G__"}` → **AGOG** ("amazed" has many synonyms; the length + the G at position 2 select it). Also fine when the prose carries more weight: *"Only spirit in the NATO phonetic alphabet"*, `{"length":7,"pattern":"___S___"}` → **WHISKEY**.
   - **rhyme_time** (`wp: {"rhyme_pair": ["<w1>","<w2>"]}`): the answer is a short rhyming phrase clued by description; the two words must genuinely rhyme (not auto-checked) but **need NOT relate in meaning** — any rhyming pair the clue can describe works. **Generally add the flipped word order as an alias** (it still rhymes), e.g. `"cat fat"` for *fat cat*, `"dare pear"` for *pear dare*. Good: *"An overweight feline"* → **fat cat**; *"A sleepover challenge to eat soft fruit"* → **pear dare**.
