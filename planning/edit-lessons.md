# Edit lessons — what human review keeps changing

Distilled from `data/acquisition/clue-edits.md` (run `mine-clue-edits.mjs` to refresh
the raw signal). These are the patterns reviewers apply over and over; **the drafter
should pre-apply them so they stop coming up.** Cross-category — value, categorization,
craft, leaks — plus wordplay style at the end.

> Maintenance: after a batch of PRs, re-run the miner and fold new recurring patterns
> in here (human-approved). Keep it tight — it's injected into the drafter's context.
>
> Backstop: an LLM editorial critic (`critique-clues.mjs`, drafter step 6c) now reviews
> every pack before the PR for the judgment-call faults the deterministic gates miss —
> tortured wording, value/difficulty mismatch, too-easy initials, signposted hidden
> words, synonym leaks. These lessons are what it looks for; pre-applying them means it
> finds nothing.

## 1. Difficulty calibration (the most-edited thing — 54 value changes)
- **A clue's difficulty is set by its single most *obvious* fact — the most recognizable path to the answer — and every lesson below is a corollary of this.** Extra facts only make a clue *easier* (each is another way in), never harder; to raise a value you **cut or obscure that most-obvious link**, you don't stack detail around it. (Clue Tom Hanks with obscure films forever — the moment "Forrest Gump" appears it's a $200 clue, and nothing else pulls it back up. And when a rewrite lands harder, the cause is almost never "it added corroborating details" — it's that it *removed a giveaway lead*.)
- **Naming the answer's famous works/achievements, or a household fact, = $200–$400 recall, not $600+.** Reviewers lowered: Colosseum, Sistine Chapel, Berlin Wall → $200; The Odyssey, 1984, The Hobbit → $200; Don Quixote, Moby-Dick → $400; Mark Twain (when it named *Tom Sawyer / Huckleberry Finn*) → $200. (2026-06-19: Queen Victoria "longest reign / Empress of India" → $200, Theodore Roosevelt "youngest president, succeeded the assassinated McKinley" → $400, Garfield → $200, Spotify → $400 — all household answers given via their famous defining fact; the drafter had them at $600–$800.) If a casual fan instantly knows it, it's cheap.
- **Earn $600–$1000 by withholding the obvious** — a single oblique/specialist hook, not a pile of famous facts. Reviewers raised: Mahler, Bayreuth, Boléro (specialist classical) → $600–$800; Battle of Ayacucho → $1000; Mark Twain *rewritten* to the Halley's-Comet hook with no book titles → $800; taiga, Enzymes → $600.
- Rule of thumb: **$200** = household name stated plainly · **$400** = one solid fact · **$600** = needs triangulation · **$800–$1000** = one oblique/counterintuitive hook, obvious anchors hidden.
- **$800–$1000 needs a genuinely DEEPER cut, not just an oblique angle on a household name** (oblique-on-marquee caps around $600 — cluing Apollo via "plague + the kouros ideal" is still only ~$600). Two routes to the top tier, both good: **(a) an obscure entry point to a famous answer** — *"Zeus's daughter, born after he took the form of a swan"* → Helen of Troy; or **(b) genuinely deep subject matter / a lesser-known answer** — *"the Greek goddess of the Moon"* → Selene; *"the Titaness of memory, mother of the nine Muses"* → Mnemosyne; Epicurus clued on ataraxia; Medusa as the only *mortal* Gorgon. **Each category pack needs its OWN hard tail — at least one $800 AND one genuine $1000 in EVERY pack, never borrowed from the sibling category drafted the same run.** (2026-06-19: both $1000s landed in history while pop culture topped out at $800 — don't let one category carry the other's depth.) Aim for roughly 3 of every ~12 clues at $800+. That depth usually means sourcing beyond the marquee topics — reach for a lesser-known figure or a specialist sub-fact, not the front-page name.
- **Span the range — keep a deliberate value MIX.** Easy $200–$400 clues are good and welcome; just don't bunch everything at one value. A strong set runs from a couple of accessible clues up through a genuine $1000.
- **Initials clues are worth LESS than they look — the initials do half the work.** (2026-06-29 / #99) A marquee two-word answer described plainly with its initials shown (Silicon Valley "the Bay Area's tech hub — S.V." → repriced $400→$200; Mount Rushmore → $200; Stephen King → $400) is a $200–$400 clue, not $600+. Two ways to justify a higher value, per §6's initials rule: make the description genuinely oblique, OR pick a less-obvious answer — otherwise price it low. Don't leave a plainly-clued famous entity at $600+ just because the initials mechanic feels clever.
- **To make a clue harder, REMOVE an identifying detail — don't add more.** Reviewers repeatedly asked exactly this: *John Adams* — "make it more challenging by removing 'second president' or even '1800'"; *Mark Twain* — drop the book titles. When a clue is comfortably gettable, cut a date/ordinal/name rather than pile on facts.
- **At higher values, a geographic or contextual tell that half-names the answer is too revealing.** *Warsaw Uprising* — "for 600, 'capital of Poland' is too revealing"; *Solidarity* — "just say 'Poland'." Scan $600+ clues for a phrase that points straight at the answer and cut it.
- **Difficulty is a property of the FACT + FRAMING, not the entity — and each CATEGORY is missing a different tier, so close its specific gap.** Before drafting, read the `tier balance` / UNDER-WEIGHT line from `category-coverage.mjs --category <id>` and weight the pack toward the flagged tiers. The flag is **bidirectional**: most categories run thin at the TOP ($800–$1000), but some run thin at the BOTTOM — music and words_language have ~1 × $200, and religion_mythology is actually top-heavy (more $800 than $400). So "balance" sometimes means writing a genuinely EASY clue, not a harder one. The same subject can be clued at any value — **Michael Jordan**: `$200` six NBA titles with the '90s Bulls · `$400` starred opposite Bugs Bunny in a live-action/animation hybrid · `$600` left for ~1.5 seasons to play minor-league baseball · `$800` the namesake of Nike's signature sneaker line · `$1000` as a UNC freshman, sank the title-game winner over Patrick Ewing's Georgetown. Pick the fact whose obscurity (and how obliquely you phrase it) genuinely lands in the tier you're filling — **never inflate an easy fact's value to hit a quota** (that's the #1 thing the 54 value-correction edits above undo). This is a gentle corrective toward evenness over time, not a flat target.

## 2. Categorize by SUBJECT, not by surface angle (8 re-homes)
A clue's category is its **answer's subject/type**, never an incidental framing:
- A **person** → their field: Annie Oakley → history/Historical Figures; Shaggy (musician) → music/Popular Music.
- A **film** → Film (not "Film Scores" unless the clue is actually about the music): The Big Lebowski → pop-culture/Film.
- A **place/city** → geography: New Orleans → geography/U.S. States & Cities.
- A **toy** → sports_games_leisure/Toys & Games (Mr. Potato Head, Easy-Bake Oven, Labubu).
- A **technology/invention** → science/Inventions & Discoveries (E-ZPass).
- A **definition/etymology/foreign phrase** → words_language, **never** filed by the linguistic angle.

## 3. Clue craft — trim, then lead with the hook (79 rewordings)
- **Cut tangential biography/context.** *Michelangelo:* "born in the Republic of Florence but spent most of his career in Rome" → "He sculpted the marble 'David' and the 'Pietà,' and painted the Sistine Chapel ceiling." Vivid, identifying detail beats encyclopedic setup.
- **Tighten.** *John Adams:* drop "permanent home / newly completed" filler. *The Odyssey:* drop the Penelope subplot — one clean sentence.
- **Cut the trailing ";…" clause.** Reviewers repeatedly lop off the second half after a semicolon — it's usually padding (*Kentucky Derby:* dropped the "colts carry 126 lb…" weights; *Backgammon:* dropped "with dice and 15 counters per side" at $600) or a leak (a "BBA vs. NBA" near-acronym tail). If a clue has a `;`, ask whether the part after it earns its place.
- **Don't telegraph with redundant geography.** *Warsaw Uprising:* "in the capital of Poland" was cut (it half-named the answer); *Solidarity:* "in a communist country" → "Polish."
- **For famous/iconic subjects, clue an oblique or surprising detail — not the textbook attribute.** Reviewers flagged mythology clues as too easy and uncreative: don't clue *Odin* as "the one-eyed Norse king of the gods" — clue his ravens Huginn and Muninn, or that he hung nine nights on Yggdrasil to win the runes. Leading with the obvious makes a would-be $600 answer play like $200. Pick the angle a casual fan *wouldn't* immediately expect. (And when a figure spans traditions, name which — "Greek" vs "Roman" — so Zeus/Jupiter isn't ambiguous.)
- **Write a clue a reader parses on the FIRST read — no convoluted, abstract, or meta phrasing, and no vague filler.** If you find yourself bending a sentence to dodge the answer's own word, find a cleaner angle instead of an awkward circumlocution. Full-clue fixes reviewers made (before → after):
  - *Argent:* "In the vocabulary of heraldry, this Old French and Latin term names the paler of the two metals permitted on a coat of arms, serving as the counterpart to 'or,' which represents gold." → "In the vocabulary of heraldry, this Old French and Latin term names the paler of the two precious metals permitted on a coat of arms."
  - *Carmen* (vague "unconventional realism"): "…this opera shocked its opening-night audience with its unconventional realism…" → "…shocked its opening-night audience by putting a defiant, free-spirited heroine and an on-stage killing before the public…"
  - *Burglar* (a back-formation framing got so tangled — "…is a back-formation: English had the noun for the person who commits the act first, and the verb was coined later by clipping that noun's ending" — that the answer was re-conceived): → "This 16th-century word for someone who breaks into buildings to steal traces back through Medieval Latin to the Latin word for a fortified town or fortress; curiously, its otherwise-unexplained 'l' may have been inserted under the influence of the Latin 'latro,' meaning thief."
- **Lead with the SUBJECT — don't bury it behind a front-loaded subordinate clause.** (2026-06-29 / #99) Reviewers called these "tortured / convoluted." A stacked preamble before "this ___" makes the clue hard to parse on the first read: *"Along with the cross, one of two punches classified as a power punch in boxing statistics, this strike is delivered from a low position…"* → **"Thrown upward from a low position toward an opponent's chin, this boxing punch is counted — along with the cross — as one of the two 'power punches'…"** (subject up front, the aside demoted). Same fix on *Three-peat*: *"Coined during the 1988–89 Los Angeles Lakers season — when the two-time defending champions sought another consecutive title — this portmanteau for a run of successive wins entered the sports lexicon."* → **"This portmanteau entered the sports lexicon during the Los Angeles Lakers' 1988–89 season, when the two-time defending champions fell short in their bid for a third straight title."** If you've opened with two commas or an em-dash aside before naming what "this" is, rewrite so the subject leads.
- **Comma economy — above all, NO comma before "and" unless it joins two INDEPENDENT clauses.** A compound predicate (one subject, two verbs) takes none: *"Suzy was a star basketball player, and a really good cook"* → *"Suzy was a star basketball player and a really good cook."* Real fix — *Prophet:* "…merchants who heeded his visions reliably found themselves generating considerable profit, and soon began bringing him offerings each morning." → "…generating considerable profit and soon began bringing him offerings each morning." Also don't wrap a restrictive clause in commas (*"this rhetorical device, in which…"* → *"this rhetorical device in which…"*). Read the clue aloud and cut any comma that isn't separating two independent clauses or list items.

## 4. Leaks & alias hygiene (recurring)
- **Never put a synonym of the answer in the clue.** "Also called mother-of-pearl" → *nacre* and "Also called the boreal forest" → *taiga* both got reworded out. Scan every clue for a word that *is* the answer by another name.
- **Person answers: add the surname as an alias** (van Gogh, Botticelli, Whistler). For visual "Name the artist," the alias is the surname; for "Name the painting," title aliases.
- **Don't alias the synonym you used as a hint** (boreal forest was removed as a taiga alias).
- **Match the clue's specificity to the exact answer, and sanity-check the grader.** Reviewers flagged: a clue that points to "Shogunate" shouldn't have answer "Tokugawa Shogunate" (clue and answer must agree on specificity); and "would the grader accept (or wrongly accept) a shorter form?" — e.g. *New Spain* vs bare "Spain." Pick the answer the clue actually pins down, and add aliases for legitimate equivalents without opening an ambiguous short answer.
- **A REWORD can re-introduce a leak — re-scan after EVERY edit for the answer's own words and any shared stem.** *Sleep apnea:* a reword to "…causes repeated pauses in breathing during sleep…" put the answer word "sleep" back into the clue (fix: "…causes breathing to repeatedly stop and restart through the night…"). *Oxide:* "…when any element chemically combines with oxygen" leaks the *ox-* stem (fix: "The binary compound that forms on the surface of a metal as it rusts or tarnishes."). Naming an etymological root that shares the stem leaks too — for *Burglar*, give the root's MEANING ("a fortified town"), never "burgus"/"borough."
- **An alias appearing verbatim in the clue is now a HARD block, not just a nudge.** (2026-06-29 / #99) A Foosball clue read "known as 'table football' in Europe" while "Table Football" was an alias — a free giveaway. The import gate now forces such a clue INACTIVE (`answer-phrase-in-clue` → `replace`), same as a primary-answer-word leak. So scan the clue against EVERY alias, not just the primary answer; if an accepted answer sits in the clue, cut it.
- **Primary answer = the SHORTEST natural form a player would say; put longer forms in aliases.** (2026-06-29 / #99) *Rose Bowl Game* → primary **"Rose Bowl"**, with "Rose Bowl Game" / "The Rose Bowl" as aliases. A player types "Rose Bowl"; making the verbose form primary risks the reveal reading oddly and the grader being needlessly strict. (This is also why the live-bank dedup now compares aliases both ways — a draft whose primary matches a live clue's alias, or vice versa, is the same clue: e.g. "Absolute pitch" vs a live "Perfect pitch"/alias-"Absolute pitch" was a dup that slipped the old answer-only gate.)
- **answer_type = 'name' ONLY for a person whose bare SURNAME should be accepted.** Mononyms and mythological/biblical figures (Heracles, Saint Christopher, Nimrod, Perseus, Plato, Cher) and monarchs whose regnal numeral is essential (Elizabeth II) are **'other'** — reviewers re-flip these every batch. Fictional people with a natural surname (Hercule Poirot, Don Draper) are fine as 'name'.

## 5. One clue per answer/work (15 drops)
- The dedupe gate now blocks identical answers, but also avoid **"effectively a repeat"**: don't clue both a work and its central character/creator (Jay Gatsby was dropped in favor of The Great Gatsby). Pick the better single clue.
- **Dedupe across the whole day's batch, not just within one pack.** A clue gets cut as "already a question/answer" when a sibling pack drafted the same day covers it — *FIFA World Cup* was dropped because the sports pack already had the Brazil/World-Cup clue; *Grand Slam* duplicated the existing tennis question. Before adding an answer, scan the other packs in the same run and the active bank.

## 6. Wordplay style (constructed clues)
Each daily wordplay pack uses **exactly two** of the seven mechanics (5 clues each) — anagram, Before & After, homophone, hidden word, initials, crossword, rhyme time; **rotate which two across runs** so anagrams + Before & After aren't the only thing that ever ships. `validate-wordplay.mjs` is the correctness gate.
- **THE RULE BEHIND ALL FOUR MECHANICS — the sentence must CLUE the answer's MEANING, not just hide its letters.** The scramble (anagram), carrier word (hidden), splice (B&A), or soundalike (homophone) is only the *mechanic*; the surrounding sentence still has to point at what the answer MEANS, or the clue is unguessable. Test every wordplay clue this way: **if you deleted the mechanic, would the sentence still nudge a solver toward the answer?** If not, rewrite the *scene* (don't just reshuffle words). An anagram of "shore" inside a sentence about dragging a dinghy gives no reason to land on *horse*; the same scramble inside a Trojan-war scene does. (2026-06-21: a reviewer called an entire anagram + hidden-word pack "effectively impossible" — every clue buried the letters in a scene unrelated to the answer.) Before→after, anagram: *"They dragged the dinghy above the tide line… on the rocky shore"* → **"The Greek warriors dragged their gift up the rocky shore and left it beneath the walls for their Trojan enemies to find."** (→ horse). Before→after, hidden word: *"The soldiers began their long march toward the capital before sunrise"* → **"The soldiers began their long march by crossing under the wall's gates well before sunrise."** (arch = the gates).
- **Anagrams — the format reviewers keep fixing:**
  - **NO fill-in-the-blank.** A clue like *"…knows when to stay completely ___"* telegraphs the answer (and the scramble word sits right beside the blank). Write a **natural sentence whose meaning the answer completes**, with the scramble word woven in elsewhere — the solver must spot which ordinary word to rearrange. Good: *"Don't leave your arm dangling below the table — just bend the hinge joint to wave hello"* → elbow (scramble "below").
  - **Never call the word out** — no bold, CAPS, italics, underline, or quotes. `constraint_text`: "Rearrange the letters of one word in this clue to find the answer" — it must not point at the word.
  - **Fresh, non-trivial pairs.** Don't reuse a pair already in any `wordplay-*.json` (silent/listen, night/thing were reused — check first). Avoid **near-synonym** pairs (angered/enraged, alerting/triangle that just sound clever) — they're too easy; the answer should be a genuine surprise, not a restatement.
- **Before & After — the two halves must be CONCEPTUALLY UNRELATED (2026-07-04/05, the #1 B&A failure the drafter + critic kept missing).** They're joined ONLY by a coincidental shared pivot word, never by a shared theme — the surprise IS that two unrelated things overlap on a word. Before writing, ask: *would these two phrases ever co-occur outside the pun?* If they share a domain it isn't a real Before & After. Reviewer cuts: *Sugar Cane* + *Cane Toad* (both belong to the cane-toad story → reworked to *Cane Toad* + *Toadstool*, an amphibian and a fungus); *Short Circuit Board*, *Ground Floor Plan*, *Music Box Office* (same-domain halves AND the composite reads as a real standalone term — *Ground Floor Plan* is itself a real thing, so no surprise). Two tests, both must pass: (1) halves from unrelated domains; (2) the spliced composite is NOT itself a real, cluable term. Good: *Golden Gate* (bridge) + *Gate Crasher* (party guest); *Iron Curtain* (geopolitics) + *Curtain Call* (theater).
- **Before & After:** one narrative cluing both halves **without naming either**, pitched between two failure modes reviewers flag. **Too pointed** = the wording near-states a half (*"a summer scoop of frozen dairy dessert meets the spreadable dairy topping"* → reworded to *"…finds itself spread upon a New York sesame specialty"* for Ice Cream Cheese). **Too obtuse** = the wording is so oblique it's unparseable (*Trolley Problem Child* was called "too obtuse," rewritten to *"A bratty imp faces an ethical dilemma aboard a small train"*). Aim for an evocative scene that *implies* each half. Good: *"An uninvited wedding guest crosses San Francisco's iconic suspension bridge on her way to the ceremony"* → Golden Gate Crasher. **Like homophones, never describe the splice mechanic in the prose** — no *"shares its key word with,"* no *"X meets Y"* enumeration; write one flowing sentence where the pivot word does double duty. (2026-06-20: a *Power Plant Food* clue literally said "shares its key word with the granular fertilizer" → rewrite to flow: *"The electricity-generating facility anchoring an industrial district produces fertilizer that helps your vegetable patch thrive."*) **The connector is usually SUBTLER than "shares a key word" and still fails** — *"…lends its final word to…," "…finds its culinary cousin in…," "…borrows its guiding principle from…," "…lands precisely on…"* all bolt two separately-described things together. The real rule: write ONE scene with ONE subject in which the pivot word does double duty — NOT *"[describe half A] [connector] [describe half B]."* (2026-06-20, **all five** B&A in one pack needed this fix: *Solar Panel Discussion* "…lends its final word to the organized forum…" → "The senators had a vigorous debate concerning the clean-energy devices mounted on rooftops worldwide"; *Cold Shoulder Pad* "…lands precisely on the foam-cushioned equipment…" → "The quarterback's teammates ostracize him after he threw a key interception, forcing him to take his equipment off by himself.")
- **Homophones — the #1 thing reviewers fix here: never announce the device.** The answer sounds like a differently-spelled word; write ONE natural scene that invokes BOTH words and let the solver *hear* the overlap. Cut every meta-phrase that points at the trick — *"the same sound refers to," "sounds phonetically identical," "the long-eared Aesop champion," "— which is precisely what this word means,"* or restating the answer's definition. **Prefer a LESS-obvious sense** of the soundalike (clue *bore* as boring a hole into wood, not "a tedious person everyone avoids"). Confirm they truly sound alike — the validator can't. (2026-06-20 reviewer rewrites, all removing the announcement: **hare/hair** → *"The barber washes and trims the hair of an overconfident racer while his slow and steady competitor trods on by"*; **bore/boar** [$800] → *"The bristly wild boar… stuck its tusk in the trunk of a large tree"* (the drilling sense); **steak/stake** "too direct" → *"…drives a wooden stake through the heart of a monster and enjoys a prime cut for dinner"*; **weak/week** → drop the trailing *"— which is precisely what this word means."*) **A second failure mode, distinct from announcing the device: "too pointed" — the scene DESCRIBES the answer directly (2026-07-05, 4 of 5 homophones reworked).** Write a scene, don't define the answer (same rule as hidden words). *"this spinning arrow atop barns indicates wind direction"* → vane over-describes exactly what a weather vane does; instead: *"Too vain to ever hold still, the copper rooster on the barn's peak swung to face each new gust off the fields"* → **vane**. *"the long-stalked allium prized in Welsh cuisine"* → leek is a definition; instead: *"A leak in the galley ceiling dripped onto the cutting board where the cook was chopping his vegetables"* → **leek**.
- **Hidden words:** the answer hides as consecutive letters inside a longer word/phrase in the clue ("cat" in "location") — never visually marked. **Same link rule as anagrams (see the rule at the top of §6): the sentence must evoke the answer's MEANING, not just contain the carrier word in an unrelated scene.** (2026-06-21 reviewer rewrites: *eel* → "The fish had a slimy **feel** that surprised anyone who touched them"; *oar* → "The soaked Vikings **aboard** their longship paddled faster to reach the shore"; *pro* → "The elite chef tightened the strings of her **apron** before beginning to cook her signature dish.") Pick a carrier word whose natural sentence can also be ABOUT the answer. **But "evoke" ≠ "define" — do NOT state the answer's definition (2026-06-29 / #99).** Because the letters sit in plain sight, a clue that also defines the answer is trivial: *"each member of this six-legged insect order marches in a single-file pro**cess**ion"* → ant is too easy (it defines "ant"). Set a SCENE that could be about the answer's world, but let the hidden letters be the only real path in, and keep the carrier word itself unrelated to the answer: *"The eleph**ant** loomed like a mountain over the tiny insect scuttling between its feet"* → **ant** (evocative, undefined, carrier "elephant" unrelated).
- **Rhyme Time:** the answer is a two-word rhyming phrase; clue it as a TERSE definition of the thing and nothing else — drop every "Rhyming pair/duo/phrase/term for…" preamble (reviewers stripped all of them). *"Rhyming pair for a gleaming, shining medieval warrior clad in armor"* → **"A gleaming medieval warrior"** (Bright Knight); *"Rhyming duo for an artificial or man-made body of freshwater"* → **"An artificial body of freshwater"** (Fake Lake). The two words need NOT relate in meaning (*"a sleepover challenge to eat soft fruit"* → pear dare). Always add the FLIPPED word order as an alias (fat cat → also "cat fat").
- **Initials & Crossword — the hint is a DISAMBIGUATOR, so go VAGUER and HARDER, not the reverse (2026-07-05).** The whole point of these two categories is that the structural hint (the initials, or the length + revealed letter) does the disambiguation — which LETS the clue be vaguer than a normal clue AND requires it to be harder. The drafter's recurring failure is writing an ordinary full trivia clue and bolting a hint on: the prose already determines the answer, the hint is redundant, and it plays far too easy (reviewers knock these straight to $200 — see §1). **The test: strip the hint — the clue should have SEVERAL plausible answers; add it back — exactly one fits.** A clue that resolves to one answer even without the hint is too pointed. Examples of the mechanic working *because* the clue is vague: *"Amazed"* + `(4) _G__` → **AGOG** ("amazed" alone has many synonyms); *"An Avenger's given name — B.B."* → **Bruce Banner** (wildly ambiguous alone). Bad: *"…North America's top professional ice-hockey league — S.C."* → Stanley Cup (the prose alone screams it).
- **Crossword mechanics:** answer ≥4 letters; because the length is shown, the clue's grammar must pin the EXACT form — a plural answer needs a plural clue ("limes," not "lime"); a past-tense answer a past-tense clue ("ground," not "grind"). Every accepted alias must also fit the length + revealed letter (the validator enforces this).
- **Initials mechanics:** NEVER echo a word of the answer (a "Berlin Airlift — B.A." clue must not contain "Berlin"). **Every accepted answer AND alias must match the displayed initials** — never add an alias whose initials differ from the hint (for "Dmitri Mendeleev — D.M." a bare "Mendeleev" alias is wrong; a spelling variant like "Dmitry Mendeleev" is fine; the validator now enforces this). **Don't restate the initials framing in the clue prose** ("Given only these initials, name the…") — the constraint text already says it. (2026-06-27) **allow_duplicate does NOT apply to initials/crossword** — their answer is a normal answer, so a duplicate is a real duplicate (unlike anagram/before-after, where the same word is a genuinely new wordplay object).
- Keep the answer's own words out of the clue (the leak guard enforces it).
